When you wash clothes, particularly wool and cotton ones in hot water, they canshrink. Randall pretends (1) that thelatitudeandlongitudelines shown on maps physically exist on theEarth, (2) that they're made of some material that shrinks when washed improperly, and (3) that the lineshavebeen washed improperly, causing them to shrink. It obviously wouldn't work as shown, given that the lines would sink where they cross oceans, which are composed primarily ofsolutions of inorganic salts in waterand are liquid (though this does not entirely rule out that a"Do not tumble dry"instruction has also been ignored, causing its own set of problems). On a similar note, the latlong grid would have to be made of an incredibly strong material to be able to squeeze into the Earth's surface, while at the same time prone to shrinking when washed.

Even ignoring all of this, the shrinking grid wouldn't actually dig into the Earth: all the latitude circles (except theequator) formsmall circlesand would simply slip to the north or south upon shrinking, finding a new position where they would now fit. The longitude circles and the equator formgreat circlesand could theoretically cut into the Earth, but in practice small disturbances (e.g. from mountains and inclined terrain) would cause them to slip either westward or eastward. Both of these deformations would happen arc by arc rather than to the whole of each circle if the joins where they intersect were strongly connected.

The level of seawater wouldn't be squeezed down under the lines and bulge outwards between them. Not counting tidal effects, tsunamis, and (relatively) small surface waves, the oceans assume nearly-constant levels, so the shrunk grid would simply submerge without affecting the ocean surface. While the rock could theoretically be depressed, this would then cause the oceans to flood the affected areas, changing the coastline, which is not depicted.

Also, the lines are conceptual, not physical, things.[citation needed]There are also 360 mainmeridians, whileRandall's globe chooses to present only 18 of them (half of them invisible on the other side). All the main meridians and also all 180 latitude parallels (i.e. those with round numbers), would shrink in the same manner, leading to a much different shape to the one Randall presents here. Additionally, there are an infinite number of non-main meridians and latitude parallels that can be used on maps as well: if all of them shrank evenly in the same way, the entire surface of Earth would be compressed into a denser form, but without any bulges.

The title text refers to theInternational Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service(IERS, formerly called the International Earth Rotation Service), and the U.S. government agency theNational Geodetic Survey(NGS). Both of these establish 3-D coordinate systems for the Earth.WGS 84(World Geodetic System 1984) is one such coordinate system, with two more recent "realizations" numberedG730(the first to use GPS measurements) andG2296(the most recent update).
