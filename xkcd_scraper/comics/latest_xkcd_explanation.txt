This is the eight installment in the series ofCursed Connectorsand presents Cursed Connectors #158: Arthurian Connector. It follows2880: Sheet Bend(#46) after about 2.5 years. This is the longest break between two connectors, beating the 1.5 years between the previous two by a year.

The comic references the fables ofKing ArthurandThe Sword in the Stone, with the Arthurian Connector on one side having a sword, presumablyExcalibur, and on the other side an anvil (being the main alternative to a stone, as the object into which the famed sword is embedded, in some common variations of the story).

In Arthurian legend, whoever can remove the sword is the lawful king of Britain. Arthur is an orphan being raised in secret, and is even unaware of his own status; he notices the sword, removes it, and is proclaimed king. The sword is sometimes identified asExcalibur, although in other versions Excalibur was acquired by King Arthur from theLady of the Lake. The most familiar version of this story isThe Sword in the StonebyT. H. Whitewhich is based onLe Morte d'ArthurbySir Thomas Malory. The animatedmusicalfilm by Walt Disney is a well-known version of this fairytale based on White's book. While Arthur was the true King of Britain, this throne is now subsumed into the crown of the United Kingdom.

Randallsays this connector can only be unplugged by a "rightful heir to the British throne", which would make this very difficult to unplug. There are only about 5,000 documented individuals who aresomewherein the line of royal succession, worldwide. And it is even more problematic if it is only the current first-heir-in-line (or even the current extant true monarch) that can accomplish this task, only allowing a further successor to take on the task after more deaths (or births, or perhaps even meaningful DNA tests) significantly alter the 'top end' of the list.

Since theAct of Settlement 1701, only descendants ofSophia of Hanover, granddaughter ofJames VI, caninherit the British throne. In the title text, Randall claims that coffee shops have "a descendant of Sophia of Hanover" to help unplug the connector (as they would be a rightful heir to the British throne). This would be impossible, though, as there are hundreds of thousands of coffee shops in the world and, as noted, only about 5,000 descendants of Sophia of Hanover. But this was "unneeded", as someone descended fromUther Pendragon(the father of Arthur) tripped and helped him unplug the connector. If Uther was a real person it would not be at all exceptional for a random stranger to be descended from him, but in reality he is a character from legend who is likely an amalgamation of multiple real people and fictional embellishments.

In the original fable, the true succession was technically unknown at the time of the challenge, and certainly was not governed by the Act Of Settlement (that would not exist for about another millennium), with a combination of the politics of the time and 'might makes right' being a more typical precursor for any such assumption of power. This demonstration that the previously largely unknown/unknowing Arthur was the true king was therefore largely dependent upon everyone accepting, in the first place, the authority of the scenario that set up the performative nature of there being a sword to free from the stone/anvil. This then has the effect of persuading enough of the people, including those who might previously have entirely conflicting ideas as to who is worthy, of the 'fact' of Arthur's kingship. The situation with this connector, however, vests the authority in people who are already marked as worthy (in some manner, with respect to established legislation and/or lore), giving them something that they can dobecausethey are already an official  successor.

Furthermore, only Arthur was capable of the original sword-extraction, not even any others who (at least in the total absence of Arthur) might normally have been able to lay claim to the kingdom. Whereas it is explicitly the case that this connector may be disconnectable bymanypossible candidates, and even across multiple possible historic justifications/precedences.
